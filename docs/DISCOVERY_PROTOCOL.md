# aXIOM — Discovery Session Protocol

Version 0.1 | Status: Draft  
*Living document. To be refined after each pilot session.*

## 1. Purpose

This protocol governs the Discovery phase of the aXIOM project — the empirical fieldwork conducted with faculty at pilot institutions prior to and during tool development. It serves two functions simultaneously:

- **Methodological:** defines the epistemological status of the Discovery sessions and the standards by which their findings are considered valid.
- **Practical:** provides a replicable procedure for conducting observations, interviews, and group discussions with faculty assessors.

The Discovery phase is not user research in the product development sense. It is empirical fieldwork grounded in an interpretive, phenomenologically informed methodology — the Lebenswelt of the faculty member (their lived, pre-theoretical experience of assessment practice) is the primary source of design knowledge.

## 2. Epistemological Position

aXIOM's Discovery phase operates within an interpretively grounded Design Science Research (DSR) framework:

| Strand | Position |
|--------|----------|
| **DSR strand** | The Discovery phase produces design knowledge embedded in the construction of an artifact (the requirement schema and assessment tool). The artifact is the scholarly contribution. |
| **Interpretive strand** | Design knowledge must be grounded in the lived practice it serves. Understanding how faculty actually experience and conduct assessment — not how they report or idealise it — is the epistemological foundation of the schema design. |
| **Validation logic** | Validity is assessed through utility (does it help?) and Nachvollziehbarkeit (does it make reasoning traceable and intersubjectively discussable?) — not through statistical measurement. |
| **Scope claim** | This is a proof of concept. The contribution is the instrument design and its theoretical framing, not empirical efficacy claims. |

## 3. Methods

Three methods are used in combination. Each produces different but complementary data.

### 3.1 Teilnehmende Beobachtung (Participant Observation)

Direct observation of assessment situations — thesis defences, committee meetings, supervisor feedback sessions, grading discussions. The observer takes a non-interventionist role. Field notes are recorded immediately after each session, not during.

**What to document:**
- How faculty articulate evaluative criteria — what language they use, what distinctions they draw
- Where disagreement arises between assessors — sites where criteria are implicit and contested
- What is left unsaid — criteria that are assumed but not stated
- How programme requirements (formal documents) relate to actual assessment practice

> **Ethics note:** Observation of assessment sessions requires explicit consent from all participants. Confirm consent protocol with each pilot institution before sessions begin.

### 3.2 Leitfadeninterview (Semi-Structured Interview)

Individual interviews with faculty assessors, conducted after observation sessions where possible. Guided by a topic list (Leitfaden), not a fixed questionnaire.

**Core interview topics:**
- "When you assess a student thesis, what are you actually looking for?" — open, non-leading
- "What makes the difference between a good thesis and an adequate one?" — elicits implicit criteria
- "Are there aspects of your assessment that are difficult to put into words?" — targets tacit knowledge
- "How do the programme requirements relate to what you actually assess?" — surfaces the gap between formal and real criteria
- "What would a tool that helps you assess look like — or not look like?" — open invitation, no product pitch

> **Note:** Do not mention aXIOM or describe the tool during interviews. The goal is to understand practice as it is, not to validate a pre-existing design.

### 3.3 Gruppendiskussion (Group Discussion)

A facilitated discussion with 3–6 faculty members from the same programme or department. The facilitator introduces a topic and then steps back, allowing the group to develop its own discourse.

**Discussion prompts:**
- Present an anonymised student thesis excerpt — ask the group to assess it collectively
- "What would you need to know about this programme to assess this properly?"
- "Where do you agree in your assessment — and where do you disagree?"

**What to document:**
- Points of spontaneous consensus — implicit shared norms
- Points of productive disagreement — contested or underdefined criteria
- The vocabulary the group uses — recurring terms are candidates for schema categories

## 4. Data & Documentation

| Type | Protocol |
|------|----------|
| **Field notes** | Written immediately after each observation. Structure: date, institution, session type, duration, participants (anonymised), key observations, open questions. |
| **Interview records** | Audio recording with consent, or written notes where recording is not permitted. Thematic summary sufficient at PoC stage. |
| **Gruppendiskussion record** | Audio recording with consent. Key passages transcribed. Focus on consensus/disagreement moments and recurring vocabulary. |
| **Schema mapping log** | After each session: document which schema categories were confirmed, challenged, or newly suggested. This is the bridge between fieldwork and design. |

## 5. Validation Criteria

### 5.1 Utility

Faculty report (or demonstrate through use) that the tool helps them articulate assessment criteria that were previously implicit or difficult to express.

### 5.2 Nachvollziehbarkeit (Traceability)

The tool makes assessment reasoning traceable — a third party reading the structured output can understand why a judgment was made, even if they disagree with it.

## 6. Scope & Limitations

- This protocol governs the PoC phase only.
- Findings are context-specific — they describe assessment practice at the pilot institutions, not assessment practice in general.
- The observer/interviewer is also the tool designer — this positionality must be declared explicitly in any publication.
- The analytical engine is a proof of concept, not a validated methodology. This framing must be consistent across all communications.
