The audit maps three traditions against each other:

1. The engine's cultural-science and qualitative analysis orientation (stated in `ANALYTICAL_COMMITMENTS.md`)
2. Design Science Research (DSR) as framed by Hevner et al. (2004)
3. Phenomenological / interpretivist grounding (Husserl, Schütz, Flick, Bohnsack)

## 1. The Engine's Stated Orientation

`ANALYTICAL_COMMITMENTS.md` opens with the following declaration:

> This document defines the methodological commitments of the aXIOM engine, with a focus on **cultural-science and qualitative analysis**.

The engine's operational commitments are:

- Outputs are **structured analytical support**, not truth claims
- All interpretive outputs are **proposals** requiring human critique
- Interpretations must never be presented as objective facts
- A strict **description / interpretation layering** — extraction precedes reading
- **Contestability by default** — counter-readings must be surfaced
- **Explicit uncertainty** — confidence markers shown, not hidden
- Strict **evidence anchoring** — no claim without a referenced segment

These commitments align with the epistemological assumptions of the cultural-science (*Kulturwissenschaften*) tradition: meaning is constructed, not discovered; interpretation is a positioned act; the analyst's stance must be declared and is open to challenge.

## 2. Design Science Research (DSR) — Hevner et al. (2004)

### Core epistemological claim

DSR holds that knowledge is produced by constructing artefacts that solve real-world problems. The artefact *is* the contribution. Validity is established through utility — does the artefact solve the problem for which it was designed?

DSR does not specify an ontological position regarding meaning or interpretation. It is epistemologically agnostic about what the artefact *does internally* — it concerns itself with the relationship between artefact construction and problem-solving.

### What DSR requires from aXIOM

- The aXIOM Academic Assessor (Module 4) must be identified as an artefact
- The problem it solves must be specified
- Utility must be demonstrable (through pilot use, at minimum)
- Methodological rigour means: transparent design decisions, evaluated utility, iterated refinement

DSR does **not** require the artefact's internal analytical logic to be positivist, neutral, or empirically derived. A DSR artefact can embody interpretivist, cultural-science, or qualitative methodological commitments provided those commitments are declared and evaluated.

### Key reference

Hevner, A. R., March, S. T., Park, J., & Ram, S. (2004). Design science in information systems research. *MIS Quarterly*, 28(1), 75–105.

## 3. Phenomenological / Interpretivist Grounding

### Core epistemological claim

The phenomenological tradition (Husserl, Schütz) holds that understanding human practice requires attending to the *Lebenswelt* — the lived world of practitioners. Meaning is not extracted from behaviour; it is constituted in experience. Research must begin from within the practice, not from outside it.

Applied to aXIOM: before designing an assessment artefact, the practice of academic assessment as it is *lived by faculty* must be understood on its own terms. This is the rationale for the Discovery phase.

### What phenomenological grounding requires from aXIOM

- Discovery sessions must be treated as empirical research, not informal user research
- The assessment schema must be grounded in observed practice, not designer assumption
- The analytical categories in the engine must be traceable to the *Lebenswelt* of academic assessment

Qualitative methods (Flick 2014, Bohnsack 2014) provide the operational translation: *Leitfadeninterview*, *dokumentarische Methode*, thematic analysis — each is a procedure for making lived experience analytically tractable.

### Relationship to cultural-science orientation

The cultural-science tradition and phenomenological interpretivist research share an epistemological foundation: meaning is constituted, not discovered; the observer is positioned, not neutral; analysis is a reading, not a measurement. This is not coincidental — much of the cultural-science (*Kulturwissenschaften*) tradition descends from neo-Kantian and phenomenological philosophy (Dilthey, Cassirer, Weber, Schütz).

## 4. Coherence Mapping

The following table maps the three traditions against each other across the key dimensions relevant to aXIOM.

| Dimension | Cultural-science / qualitative (engine) | DSR (Hevner et al. 2004) | Phenomenological / interpretivist |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ontology** | Meaning is constructed, context-dependent | Agnostic — artefact solves a problem | Meaning is constituted in lived experience |
| **Epistemology** | Interpretation is a positioned act | Knowledge = utility of artefact | Understanding requires inhabiting practice |
| **Validity criterion** | *Nachvollziehbarkeit*, intersubjective verifiability | Utility — does it solve the problem? | Faithfulness to lived experience (*Lebenswelt*) |
| **Role of the analyst** | Declared, positioned, contestable | Artefact designer — transparent decisions | Researcher who understands from within |
| **Output status** | Proposal for critique, not truth | Design solution, evaluated in use | Interpretation grounded in practice |
| **Neutrality** | Explicitly rejected — all frameworks are positioned | Not required — artefact can embody any stance | Bracketed via epoché, not eliminated |
| **Counter-reading** | Required by default | Not a DSR concept, but compatible | Consistent with pluralism of interpretive approaches |

### Intersection points

1. **All three reject naive objectivism.** DSR evaluates artefacts through use (not derivation from first principles). Cultural-science and phenomenology reject the positivist claim that meaning can be extracted neutrally. None of the three traditions demands or assumes a view from nowhere.

2. **Transparency is a shared value.** DSR requires transparent design decisions. Cultural-science requires declared analytical stances (§0 of the engine schema). Phenomenological qualitative research requires documented reflexivity (Flick 2014). The engine's *Nachvollziehbarkeit* criterion is the operational form of this shared commitment.

3. **The artefact is both product and knowledge.** In DSR, the artefact is the scholarly contribution. In the cultural-science tradition applied here, the schema — the structured analytical instrument — *is* the methodological contribution. These are compatible claims about the same object.

4. **Grounding in practice is required by two of the three traditions.** Phenomenology requires it directly (*Lebenswelt*). DSR requires problem specification from the field. Cultural-science applied to an assessment context requires understanding what assessment means to those who conduct it. All three point toward the same requirement: empirical grounding through the Discovery phase.

### Divergence points

1. **Validity criteria differ.** DSR's utility criterion, phenomenology's faithfulness criterion, and cultural-science's *Nachvollziehbarkeit* criterion are not identical. For academic publication, the dominant validity criterion must be named. **Resolution:** *Nachvollziehbarkeit* is proposed as the primary criterion because it is explicitly embedded in the engine's design and coherent with both interpretivist and qualitative research standards. Utility (DSR) is a secondary criterion confirming practical value. Faithfulness to lived experience (*Lebenswelt*) is satisfied through the Discovery phase protocol.

2. **DSR's stance on the artefact's internal logic is silent, not affirmative.** DSR does not validate the cultural-science orientation — it is simply agnostic about it. This means the cultural-science commitments must be justified on their own terms (through ANALYTICAL_COMMITMENTS.md and the methodological literature), not via DSR.

3. **Phenomenology operates at the level of practice; DSR operates at the level of artefact.** These are not in conflict but they address different levels of analysis. The engine coherence audit must hold both levels simultaneously: the artefact (DSR) must be grounded in lived practice (phenomenology) and must produce traceable, contestable outputs (cultural-science).

## 5. Resolution

**The engine's cultural-science and qualitative orientation is coherent with the interpretively grounded DSR framework.**

The three traditions are not in conflict. They are operating at different levels of abstraction and address complementary questions:

- **DSR** answers: *What kind of scholarly contribution is aXIOM?* → An artefact evaluated through utility.
- **Phenomenological / interpretivist grounding** answers: *On what empirical basis does the artefact claim to address assessment practice?* → On the Lebenswelt of faculty, accessed through rigorous qualitative fieldwork.
- **Cultural-science / qualitative orientation** answers: *What epistemological commitments govern the engine's analytical outputs?* → Declared interpretive stance, description/interpretation separation, evidence anchoring, contestability by default, explicit uncertainty.

The only point requiring ongoing attention is the **primary validity criterion**, which must be explicitly named before academic publication. *Nachvollziehbarkeit* is the recommended primary criterion. This must be stated explicitly in METHODOLOGY.md when Question 3 (validation logic) is resolved.

**Status: ✅ RESOLVED**

The alignment requirement from METHODOLOGY.md is closed. The engine's cultural studies orientation is coherent with interpretively grounded DSR. No architectural modification is required. The "productive tension" identified in METHODOLOGY.md (structured schema supporting interpretive judgment) is not a contradiction but the engine's core contribution.
