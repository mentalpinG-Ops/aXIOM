## Section I — Design Science Research (DSR)

*Methodological foundation for treating aXIOM and the DFS as designed artefacts subject to evaluation criteria rather than purely theoretical constructs.*

---

**Hevner, A. R., March, S. T., Park, J., & Ram, S. (2004). Design science in information systems research. *MIS Quarterly*, 28(1), 75–105.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Establishes the seven DSR guidelines (problem relevance, design artefact, design evaluation, research contributions, research rigor, design as a search process, communication of research) that inform how the DFS and the prompt library are framed as researchable artefacts rather than informal tools.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.2307/25148625

---

**Peffers, K., Tuunanen, T., Rothenberger, M. A., & Chatterjee, S. (2007). A design science research methodology for information systems research. *Journal of Management Information Systems*, 24(3), 45–77.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The DSRM process model (problem identification → objectives → design → demonstration → evaluation → communication) maps directly onto the mentalpinG-Ops pipeline from Capture through Draft to Stable and Transfer.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.2753/MIS0742-1222240302

---

**Wieringa, R. J. (2014). *Design science methodology for information systems and software engineering*. Springer.**  
🔶  
**Role in project:** Provides the treatment-investigation distinction that separates the analytical prompts (treatments) from the Discovery Sessions that evaluate their outputs (investigations). Grounds the claim that DFS-conformant prompts are investigable research artefacts.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-662-43839-8

## Section II — Phenomenology

*Grounds the L2 objects and L3 operations in the Phenomenology prompt and informs the D2 "inside / participatory" observer position used across the library.*

---

**Husserl, E. (1970). *The crisis of European sciences and transcendental phenomenology* (D. Carr, Trans.). Northwestern University Press. (Original work published 1936)**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The concept of the lifeworld (*Lebenswelt*) anchors the Phenomenology prompt's L1 commitment that consciousness is always already world-directed. The crisis diagnosis also informs the project's rationale: analytical frameworks that leave presuppositions implicit reproduce the crisis Husserl described.  
**Note:** Northwestern University Press Studies in Phenomenology and Existential Philosophy series.

---

**Heidegger, M. (1962). *Being and time* (J. Macquarrie & E. Robinson, Trans.). Harper & Row. (Original work published 1927)**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The ready-to-hand / present-at-hand distinction and the analysis of *Dasein* as being-in-the-world supply the ontological underpinning for the Phenomenology prompt's treatment of practical engagement as prior to detached observation. Informs D2 "inside" observer position.

---

**Merleau-Ponty, M. (2012). *Phenomenology of perception* (D. Landes, Trans.). Routledge. (Original work published 1945)**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The body-subject analysis and the doctrine of motor intentionality ground the Phenomenology prompt's L2 object "embodied perception." Connects to Poststructuralism via Foucault's genealogy of the body and to Feminist Theory via Beauvoir's corporeal situatedness.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.4324/9780203720714

---

**van Manen, M. (1990). *Researching lived experience: Human science for an action sensitive pedagogy*. State University of New York Press.**  
🔶  
**Role in project:** The four existential lifeworld dimensions (lived space, lived body, lived time, lived human relation) provide the structural vocabulary used in the Phenomenology prompt's L3 operations when the analysis is applied to qualitative research data rather than philosophical texts.

## Section III — Qualitative Research Methods

*Epistemological and procedural foundations for the Discovery Session methodology and the Content Analysis (Inhaltsanalyse) prompt.*

---

**Mayring, P. (2015). Qualitative content analysis: Theoretical background and procedures. In A. Bikner-Ahsbahs, C. Knipping, & N. Presmeg (Eds.), *Approaches to qualitative research in mathematics education* (pp. 365–380). Springer.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The deductive and inductive category formation procedures in the Content Analysis prompt follow Mayring's Inhaltsanalyse methodology directly. The step-by-step coding model (category system → material pass → frequency analysis or summarisation) is the primary methodological referent for the Qualitative Content Analysis prompts.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/978-94-017-9181-6_13

---

**Kuckartz, U. (2018). *Qualitative text analysis: A systematic approach*. In G. Kaiser & N. Presmeg (Eds.), *Compendium for early career researchers in mathematics education* (pp. 181–197). Springer.**  
🔶  
**Role in project:** Supplies the structuring content analysis variant (kategorienbasierte Auswertung) used when the prompt is applied to corpus-level material. Kuckartz's framework extends Mayring's procedures to mixed-method and computer-assisted contexts.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-030-15636-7_8

---

**Flick, U. (2018). *An introduction to qualitative research* (6th ed.). SAGE Publications.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Provides the epistemological framing for the choice of qualitative over quantitative methods in Discovery Sessions. The triangulation framework (methodological, theoretical, investigator) maps onto the multi-framework analysis mode used in the library.

---

**Creswell, J. W., & Creswell, J. D. (2018). *Research design: Qualitative, quantitative, and mixed methods approaches* (5th ed.). SAGE Publications.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The philosophical worldviews framework (postpositivism, constructivism, transformative, pragmatism) is used in the SCHEMA.md section on DFS methodological neighbourhood to position the prompt library relative to competing research paradigms.

## Section IV — Human-Centered Design (HCD)

*Informs the Discovery Session structure and the triage prompt's design as a user-facing analytical instrument.*

---

**Norman, D. A. (2013). *The design of everyday things* (Revised and expanded ed.). Basic Books.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The affordance / constraint / mapping triad from Norman is the implicit design vocabulary for the DFS prompt structure: each prompt must afford the operation it is designed to produce, constrain outputs to framework-specific categories, and map the analytical steps to the analyst's mental model of the framework.

---

**ISO 9241-210. (2019). *Ergonomics of human-system interaction — Part 210: Human-centred design for interactive systems* (2nd ed.). International Organization for Standardization.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The HCD process (understanding context of use → specifying requirements → producing design solutions → evaluating against requirements) structures the production-ready criteria in PROMPT_GOVERNANCE.md: a prompt is production-ready only when it has been evaluated against user requirements in at least one Discovery Session.  
**URL:** https://www.iso.org/standard/77520.html

---

**Sanders, E. B.-N., & Stappers, P. J. (2008). Co-creation and the new landscapes of design. *CoDesign*, 4(1), 5–18.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The expert–participant–co-designer continuum grounds the Discovery Session's role: users are not passive test subjects but co-producers of the analytical schema validation. This framing underpins the "output confirmed by user" criterion in the transfer pipeline.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/15710880701875068

## Section V — Communities of Practice (CoP)

*Provides the social learning theory that frames the mentalpinG-Ops / aXIOM two-repository model as a practice-based knowledge commons rather than a document archive.*

---

**Wenger, E. (1998). *Communities of practice: Learning, meaning, and identity*. Cambridge University Press.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The three structural features of a CoP (mutual engagement, joint enterprise, shared repertoire) map directly onto the prompt-library project: mutual engagement is the Discovery Session and the review process; joint enterprise is the production of DFS-conformant analytical instruments; the shared repertoire is the library itself (prompts, schema, governance documents).  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511803932

---

**Lave, J., & Wenger, E. (1991). *Situated learning: Legitimate peripheral participation*. Cambridge University Press.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Legitimate peripheral participation (LPP) describes how a new analyst moves from using the library as a novice to contributing as a co-developer — the Capture → Draft → Stable pipeline enacts the LPP trajectory.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511815355

---

**Wenger, E., McDermott, R., & Snyder, W. M. (2002). *Cultivating communities of practice*. Harvard Business School Press.**  
🔶  
**Role in project:** The chapter on knowledge domains and the rhythm of community life informs the rationale for separating mentalpinG-Ops (development and cultivation) from aXIOM (deployment and production). The two-repo model enacts the workshop/product distinction Wenger et al. identify as a structural feature of mature CoPs.

## Section VI — Semiotics

*Foundational sources for the Semiotics prompt and for the DFS's own conceptual neighbourhood (Morris's semiotic strata).*

---

**Saussure, F. de (1983). *Course in general linguistics* (R. Harris, Trans.). Duckworth. (Original work published 1916)**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The dyadic sign model (signifier / signified), the langue / parole distinction, and the synchronic / diachronic split ground the Semiotics prompt's L1 commitments and D3 temporality declaration. The SCHEMA.md cites Saussure's strata explicitly in the DFS methodological neighbourhood section.

---

**Barthes, R. (1972). *Mythologies* (A. Lavers, Trans.). Cape. (Original work published 1957)**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Barthes's second-order semiological system (myth as metalanguage) is the primary source for the Myth Analysis prompt. The demythologising operation is the L3 referent for "naturalisation → historicisation." The ironic observer position (D2) in the Semiotics prompt is explicitly grounded in Barthes's practice.

---

**Peirce, C. S. (1931–1958). *Collected papers of Charles Sanders Peirce* (Vols. 1–8, C. Hartshorne, P. Weiss, & A. Burks, Eds.). Harvard University Press.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The triadic sign model (representamen / object / interpretant) and the icon / index / symbol taxonomy supply the L2 objects for the Semiotic decomposition prompt. The conflict between Peircean and Saussurean sign ontologies is declared as a Conflicts entry in the Semiotics prompt file.

---

**Eco, U. (1976). *A theory of semiotics*. Indiana University Press.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Eco's theory of codes and the concept of unlimited semiosis inform the L3 operations for code identification and semiosis tracing. The conflict between unlimited semiosis (Peirce / Eco) and structuralist closure is documented in the Semiotics prompt's Conflicts section.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-349-15849-2

---

**Hall, S. (1980). Encoding/decoding. In S. Hall, D. Hobson, A. Lowe, & P. Willis (Eds.), *Culture, media, language* (pp. 128–138). Hutchinson.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Hall's three reading positions (dominant / negotiated / oppositional) extend the Semiotics prompt to ideologically situated reception. Grounds the D2 "engaged" observer variant and provides the Interop hook connecting Semiotics to Critical Theory and Postcolonial Theory.

## Section VII — AI Ethics

*Grounds the ethical framing of the DFS as a governance instrument and informs the Section XI AI Governance sources.*

---

**Floridi, L., Cowls, J., Beltrametti, M., Chatila, R., Chazerand, P., Dignum, V., Luetge, C., Madelin, R., Pagallo, U., Rossi, F., Schafer, B., Valcke, P., & Vayena, E. (2018). AI4People — An ethical framework for a good AI society: Opportunities, risks, principles, and recommendations. *Minds and Machines*, 28(4), 689–707.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The five AI4People principles (beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, justice, explicability) inform the ethical controls in the prompt architecture. The explicability principle grounds the DFS requirement for explicit L1 declaration: a prompt that does not declare its presuppositions violates explicability.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s11023-018-9482-5

---

**Jobin, A., Ienca, M., & Vayena, E. (2019). The global landscape of AI ethics guidelines. *Nature Machine Intelligence*, 1(9), 389–399.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The convergence analysis (transparency, justice/fairness, non-maleficence, responsibility, privacy) contextualises the DFS governance model within the broader field of AI ethics guidance. Confirms that transparency / explicability is the most widely endorsed principle across global frameworks — consistent with the DFS's core design.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s42256-019-0088-2

---

**Bender, E. M., Gebru, T., McMillan-Major, A., & Shmitchell, S. (2021). On the dangers of stochastic parrots: Can language models be too big? In *FAccT '21: Proceedings of the 2021 ACM Conference on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency* (pp. 610–623). ACM.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The stochastic parrot critique — that LLMs produce fluent but semantically unmoored outputs — is the direct problem the DFS addresses at the prompt level. The risk of AI-generic outputs that the PROMPT_GOVERNANCE.md identifies as a blocking criterion for production-readiness is the practical operationalisation of this critique.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/3442188.3445922

---

**Mittelstadt, B. D., Allo, P., Taddeo, M., Wachter, S., & Floridi, L. (2016). The ethics of algorithms: Mapping the debate. *Big Data & Society*, 3(2), 1–21.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The six categories of ethical concern (inconclusive evidence, inscrutable evidence, misguided evidence, unfair outcomes, transformative effects, traceability) map onto the DFS Conflicts section function: conflict declarations identify where a prompt's algorithmic operation risks producing ethically misguided evidence.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1177/2053951716679679

## Section VIII — AI Hallucination

*Grounds the project's treatment of AI output reliability and the "output confirmed by user" criterion in the transfer pipeline.*

---

**Ji, Z., Lee, N., Frieske, R., Yu, T., Su, D., Xu, Y., Ishii, E., Bang, Y., Madotto, A., & Fung, P. (2023). Survey of hallucination in natural language generation. *ACM Computing Surveys*, 55(12), 1–38.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The hallucination taxonomy (intrinsic — contradicts the source; extrinsic — unverifiable against the source) informs the validation criteria in PROMPT_GOVERNANCE.md: "output confirmed" means that the output can be evaluated against an independent standard, not merely that it sounds coherent.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1145/3571730

---

**Huang, L., Yu, W., Ma, W., Zhong, W., Feng, Z., Wang, H., Chen, Q., Peng, W., Feng, X., Qin, B., & Liu, T. (2023). A survey on hallucination in large language models: Principles, taxonomy, challenges, and open questions. *arXiv preprint arXiv:2311.05232*.**  
🔶  
**Role in project:** The factuality / faithfulness distinction and the mitigation taxonomy (retrieval-augmented, chain-of-thought, self-refinement) contextualise the DFS approach: DFS-conformant prompts address hallucination not through retrieval but through structural constraint — the framework's L1/L2/L3 declarations constrain the output space.  
**URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.05232

---

**Maynez, J., Narayan, S., Bohnet, B., & McDonald, R. (2020). On faithfulness and factuality in abstractive summarization. In *ACL 2020: Proceedings of the 58th Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics* (pp. 1906–1919). Association for Computational Linguistics.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The faithfulness / factuality distinction in summarisation is the analogue of the DFS's validity / soundness distinction: a prompt output can be faithful to the framework's operations (formally valid) while resting on unverified premises (factually unsound). The transfer pipeline's separate "output confirmed" and "scope-compliant" criteria reflect this distinction.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.18653/v1/2020.acl-main.173

## Section IX — Knowledge Commons

*Theoretical foundation for the CC BY-SA 4.0 licensing model and the two-repository governance architecture.*

---

**Ostrom, E. (1990). *Governing the commons: The evolution of institutions for collective action*. Cambridge University Press.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Ostrom's eight design principles for stable common-pool resource institutions (clearly defined boundaries, congruence with local conditions, collective choice arrangements, monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict-resolution mechanisms, recognition of rights, nested enterprises) map onto the mentalpinG-Ops governance model. The two-repo architecture (workshop / product) reflects the nested enterprise principle.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511807763

---

**Hess, C., & Ostrom, E. (Eds.). (2007). *Understanding knowledge as a commons: From theory to practice*. MIT Press.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The knowledge commons framework extends Ostrom's model from physical to intellectual resources. The chapter on digital knowledge commons directly informs the rationale for CC BY-SA 4.0 rather than a more restrictive licence: the prompt library is designed as a knowledge commons, not a proprietary product.  
**URL:** https://direct.mit.edu/books/book/2843/Understanding-Knowledge-as-a-CommonsFrom-Theory-to

---

**Benkler, Y. (2006). *The wealth of networks: How social production transforms markets and freedom*. Yale University Press.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** The commons-based peer production model grounds the decision to make the mentalpinG-Ops library openly accessible and to separate development (mentalpinG-Ops) from deployment (aXIOM). The network effects of open contribution are a design assumption in the CC BY-SA 4.0 model.  
**URL:** https://cyber.harvard.edu/wealth_of_networks

---

**Lessig, L. (2004). *Free culture: How big media uses technology and the law to lock down culture and control creativity*. Penguin Press.**  
🔶  
**Role in project:** Provides the free-culture rationale for the ShareAlike condition in CC BY-SA 4.0. The lock-down critique motivates the explicit choice of a copyleft licence over Creative Commons licences that permit non-commercial restrictions.

## Section X — Schema Foundations

*Intellectual antecedents of the Declarative Frame Schema (DFS), as documented in SCHEMA.md.*

---

**Marr, D. (1982). *Vision: A computational investigation into the human representation and processing of visual information*. W. H. Freeman.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Marr's three levels (computational / algorithmic / implementational) are the most cited antecedent of the DFS L1/L2/L3 decomposition. SCHEMA.md distinguishes the DFS from Marr: the DFS is a governance schema for prompt design, not a theory of cognitive implementation, but the structural parallel is acknowledged and the distinction declared.

---

**Morris, C. W. (1938). *Foundations of the theory of signs* (International Encyclopedia of Unified Science, Vol. 1, No. 2). University of Chicago Press.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Morris's tripartite semiotic strata (syntactic / semantic / pragmatic) are identified in SCHEMA.md as a methodological neighbour of the DFS descriptive dimensions (D1–D3). The DFS is not derived from Morris, but both share the structural concern with making explicit the levels at which an analytical operation is calibrated.

---

**Fillmore, C. J. (1976). Frame semantics and the nature of language. *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 280(1), 20–32.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Fillmore's frame semantics — the idea that words evoke structured knowledge schemas (frames) that determine what counts as relevant background — is the implicit reference point for the DFS name: a "Declarative Frame" in the DFS sense is an explicit statement of the cognitive frame a prompt activates. Declaring the frame is the alternative to allowing it to operate covertly.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1749-6632.1976.tb25467.x

---

**Minsky, M. (1975). A framework for representing knowledge. In P. H. Winston (Ed.), *The psychology of computer vision* (pp. 211–277). McGraw-Hill.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Minsky's frame theory introduced the slot-and-filler structure that underlies schema-based knowledge representation in AI. The DFS eight-component structure can be understood as a schema in Minsky's sense: each component is a slot that must be filled before the prompt is DFS-conformant. Historically situates the DFS within the frame-representation tradition.

---

**Goffman, E. (1974). *Frame analysis: An essay on the organization of experience*. Harvard University Press.**  
✅  
**Role in project:** Goffman's sociological frame analysis — how participants use frameworks (primary frameworks) to interpret strips of activity — provides the social-interactional complement to Fillmore's linguistic frame semantics. Together they establish that framing is always already occurring; the DFS makes the frame explicit rather than suppressing it.

## Section XI — AI Governance & Prompt Architecture

*Sources directly informing the DFS as a governance instrument and the two-repository prompt architecture. All entries in this section have been added in v1.0 (2026-04-21) and were not present in the aXIOM bibliography.*

---

**Nabben, K. (2024). KMS-GPT: Knowledge management, self-infrastructuring, and local subsidiarity in AI-assisted organisations. BlockScience.**  
⚠️ Unverified — details drawn from project notes; full publication reference requires independent confirmation.  
**Role in project:** The KMS-GPT framing introduces the concept of self-infrastructuring: communities that build and govern their own AI knowledge infrastructure rather than depending on external platforms. The local subsidiarity principle — decisions should be made at the lowest competent level — grounds the mentalpinG-Ops design choice to develop prompts locally and transfer only production-ready artefacts to aXIOM. Informs the two-repo model's jurisdictional logic.  
**DOI/URL:** ⚠️ To be confirmed — see BlockScience publications index.

---

**Role in project:** The three-layer governance architecture (infrastructure / analytical / verification) maps onto the DFS pipeline: the DFS schema is the analytical layer; the taxonomy-review-schema and the production-ready criteria constitute the verification layer; the mentalpinG-Ops repository is the infrastructure layer. The verification layer concept directly informed the "output confirmed by user" criterion in PROMPT_GOVERNANCE.md.  
**DOI/URL:** ⚠️ To be confirmed.

This gap is the direct problem the DFS addresses: explicit L1 declaration is the governance mechanism that closes the gap identified in this paper.  
**DOI/URL:** ⚠️ arXiv ID to be confirmed.
